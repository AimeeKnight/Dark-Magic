Next up, let's talk about working smart.
Everyone knows the saying, ‘Failing to plan is planning to fail’.
Spend time thinking before coding. Ex: API => start with Docs.
Have an opinion... be able to explain why you made the choices you made! Nothing is more painful than seeing code that's been C&P'd and you know it.
Understand every line of code you write. If it’s there, it should have a purpose.
With my background in skating and coaching, I know first hand that practice does NOT make perfect... it makes permanent.
Focus less on being a great developer, more on being great problem solver.
With that said, you should be reflecting on your work regularly.
When you're working on a tough problem, and you finally come up with a solution, step back, and retrace your steps.
Write things down even.

(FADE IN) You'll help Jr's on your team immensely, if you frame conversations accordingly.
Which means explain things differently to a Jr than you would a Sr.
A lot of the time Jr's don’t have the same context to rely on.
If you and I were watching the Olympics together and I told you 'a skater cheated their double axel & had a wrap’, you’d have no idea.
Instead, I could say to you 'they rotated their foot a 1/4 turn before taking off (cheating),
and their leg went above their knee in the air (wrapping) which is poor style’.
I might even get up and draw… hint!
With the inverse approach:
You might not know what a double axel is, so you Google that,
then you have to figure out what the heck ‘cheated’ and ‘wrap’ mean.. in that context.
Before you know it, what could be explained quickly, turned into a 30 minute rabbit hole for you.
You might think this is a pain, but it'll help you grow as you’ll have to think about problems in a different way.
(FADE IN) Project mgmt. is another subtle, but extremely crucial step.
Managers & product managers should work together to ensure projects flow smoothly.
Jr's are still getting used to being able to gauge timing on projects, and will probably need time before they're able to give solid estimates.
Ambiguity can be tough to deal with too. So, you need to set explicit expectations.
Provide additional details or constraints & break tasks down. Doing so will ultimately help a new developer contribute faster.
I've also seen the inverse, where Jr's aren't given enough to do.
It's easy to resort to having a Jr's sit idle, or to never be delegated substantial tasks.

(FADE IN) Next, I want to talk about code reviews.
Can either be an awesome way to learn, or extremely painful and discouraging.
Don't let this happen. With a Jr, every review is an opportunity to teach them something.
Unless you want your dog (air quotes) to wear a muzzle the rest of their life, resist the urge to do this too.
Don’t just tell a Jr what to do. Ask them their thoughts on doing something in the way you propose.
It forces them to think, and come up with own thoughts.
After, tell them ‘why’.
Offer's more technical value & build trust.
Next, pair programming.
#1 Thing: Tell the Jr when you’re not sure. Seriously... please!
sets an example for humility, and NOT doing so will eventually come back to bite the team.
Don’t get too clever with your solutions.
If it was hard to write, it’ll be even harder to read in the future!
Debug pair programming can be immensely valuable
Whether people admit or not, everyone writes bugs.
More often than not, we're working on existing code bases.
Learning strategies to quickly pin point issues in code you didn't write is arguably more difficult than writing your own program from scratch.
With that said, next time you’re debugging grab the Jr on your team and walk through the process with them.
